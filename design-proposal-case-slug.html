Case 01 — MyLINK Portal

Unifying 50+ legacy products
into one platform

From the inside out — a multi-year journey through organisational change, product strategy, and platform design.

Product Strategy UX Leadership Organisational Change Go-to-Market Platform Design

LINK Mobility is one of Europe's largest CPaaS providers — Oslo-listed, ~700 employees, NOK 7bn revenue, 50,000+ customers sending over 20 billion messages a year. It had grown primarily through acquisitions, resulting in a fragmented product landscape that was slowly becoming a liability — technically, commercially, and organisationally.

The Situation

Dozens of products, dozens of teams, dozens of roadmaps — none of them talking to each other. Customers were managing four or five different portals to access services that should have been unified years ago. Sales teams were struggling to pitch a coherent story. Engineering was maintaining legacy systems with no clear path forward.

The business case for unification was obvious to everyone. What wasn't obvious was who owned it, how to sequence it, and how to bring along the people who had built their identities around the products being consolidated.

This wasn't primarily a design challenge. It was a change management challenge dressed up as a product challenge. That distinction shaped every decision I made over the next four years.

How I Approached It
01

The Copenhagen moment

The first leadership alignment session was going nowhere. Fourteen people around a table, each defending their product's strategic importance. I asked one question: "If a new customer signed with us tomorrow and we could only give them one access point — what would it be?" The room went quiet. That question unlocked six months of subsequent agreement.

"In politically charged rooms, a well-placed question does more than any argument."
02

Build the north star before anything else

Before wireframes, before engineering sprints, before any feature discussion — I wrote the Product Blueprint. A 40-page document defining the platform's purpose, principles, scope, and the sequencing logic for every migration phase. It became the reference document that every team pointed to when scope debates arose. Three years later, it still is.

The Blueprint wasn't a backlog. It was a strategic anchor. The difference matters: a backlog can be negotiated away line by line. A blueprint forces conversations about direction, not just features.

03

Build the team before building the product

I built the UX practice from scratch: process definition, hiring criteria, onboarding structure, rituals. Five designers across three countries. Zero turnover in four years — in an industry where 18-month tenures are considered long.

The team's stability became the product's stability. Institutional knowledge didn't walk out the door. Patterns were refined over time rather than reinvented. The consistency in the final product is a direct output of the consistency in the team.

What I Learned

The hardest part wasn't the product — it was the politics. Every meaningful change required building trust in three directions simultaneously: with the engineering team who doubted PM involvement, with the market managers who feared disruption, and with C-level stakeholders who had heard promises before.

What I underestimated at the start was how much of product leadership is reputation management. Your ability to ship depends on the credit you've accumulated with the people around you. You spend it every time you ask for a hard call. You build it every time you tell the truth when a comfortable lie was available.

"Credibility under pressure is built by telling the truth when it's uncomfortable."
